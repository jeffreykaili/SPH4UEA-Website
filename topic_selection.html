Topic Selection

Narrowing down the options...

Posted on September 27, 2021

Although I am interested in all the topics from my brainstorming, I narrowed it down to two main ideas based on my specific skills and passions. Computer science is where I excel the most, and I think it would make sense to build my PPP around that. This also gives me room to go above and beyond and explore different avenues that I wouldn't be able to do with the other topics because of the lack of comfort. Looking at my conclusions:

Space Flight/Astronautics - Rejected
  • Alongside being an expensive idea, I've already built a rocket before (given it was built blindly without any real consideration for physics). Although this is a school assignment, I want to do something new because the unfamiliarity allows for curiosity which drives me to learn. Also, redoing something I've done before reduces the fun :(
Astrophysics - Rejected
  • From the brainstorming, it was pretty evident that this was not a topic I was well-versed in. Space and astrophysics interest me, and that is why they ended up as potential topics. It's such a broad field that I have no experience or knowledge in, and I was hoping for an idea that would have prompted an experiment/final product or even narrowed the topic down, but since nothing came, I think I'll set this idea down for now.
Wireless Charging - Rejected
  • Wireless charging was an idea I was considering because it is a real-world application of magnetic fields. However, reflecting on this idea, it is not a topic that particularly interests me. I feel like my lack of interest would result in a sub-par project, and thus, I want to select a topic that I am passionate about.
The Physics of Flight - Rejected
  • Looking back, this idea was pretty similar to rocketry. I thought one benefit would be that it was cheaper (?) than rocketry, and I would be able to go through more models. However, if I'm not working with paper airplanes (I already did "the best paper airplane design" in grade 6), the cost would not be much cheaper, if at all. It's still a pretty appealing topic, but factoring in the prices and the fact that other classmates have already selected flight as their idea, I'm going to go for something else.

Accepted Topics

Out of the six topics I brainstormed originally, it came down to these two.

Robotics/Some Sort of Machine - Accepted
  • My idea for this topic was to create a program that could predict the landing of a ball using two cameras - one would be positioned in front of the ball, and one would be positioned to the side. The camera on the side would capture the x/y-axis movement, and the front camera would capture the z-axis movement.
A rough sketch of what each camera would capture to predict the ball's trajectory.
  • Using this information, values such as the velocity, acceleration, and height above the ground could be calculated, allowing for an estimation of where the ball would land.
  • However, I decided to not go with this idea for my PPP because:
    1. I don't have a table where I can build this machine and keep it there for four months.
    2. I don't have the two cameras I would need to track the ball.
  • Even though I do not have much experience with computer vision, this would be my choice if it was convenient. Unfortunately, I don't think it's realistic right now, but I plan to revisit it in the future.
Physics Simulations - Accepted + PPP Topic!
  • Simulating physics was the topic I selected because it was the most realistic and gave me the most freedom - I feel like I have lots of opportunities to learn and explore beyond the project. It also made sense because I wanted a topic that had real-world applications. Although I struggled to find direct connections, I spoke with Mr. Fong and he recommended that I create a physics engine - a bridge between accurately simulating physics while still providing room for minor errors. It's also less computationally intensive, and I also play plenty of video games :)

Answering Brainstorm Questions

  • Research Question/Goal:
    • How is physics efficiently simulated in video games while also appearing realistic to the players?
  • What question(s) do you have?
    • How do physics engines work? What are the specific components that allow it to simulate real-life physics?
      • How are key features such as motion, forces, collision detection, and collision response implemented in code?
    • What trade-offs are made to improve performance for a real-time physics engine? In other words, what specific parts of physics are simulated less accurately?
    • How do these game engines connect to more realistic simulations?
    • What are the computational limitations of physics simulation?
  • How do you plan on answering the question(s)?:
    • I plan on conducting both secondary and primary research to answer these questions.
      • In my secondary research, I'll learn about popular physics engines, their implementations, the components of simulation, and accuracy vs. speed. As my secondary research provides background for my primary research, I’ll also be looking into how to create a physics engine from scratch.
      • In my primary research, I'll use what I've learned from my secondary research to create my own 2D equation-based physics engine!
  • Demo/product ideas?:
    • My final product will be a completed 2D physics engine including:
      • Motion (Kinematic Equations)
      • Forces (Newton’s three laws of motion)
      • Collision detection
      • Collision Response
  • How does your idea apply to everyday life (society/environment)?:
    • Physics simulations have a plethora of applications in our everyday lives - they enable us to model aerodynamics for cars and planes at zero cost, realistically simulate vehicles crashes, and extensively predict/model the climate.
